## 📖 LESSON 1 — Why Ratios? Comparing Apples to Apples

### 🧑‍🏫 Master Says:

You already know from Session 02 that **Company B** with ₹40 cr profit was MORE efficient than **Company A** with ₹50 cr profit — because margins matter more than raw numbers.

Ratios take this thinking further. They let you **compare any two companies fairly**, regardless of size.

### 🤔 The Problem Without Ratios:

> - Reliance Industries Net Profit: ₹79,000 crore
> - A small pharma company Net Profit: ₹800 crore
>
> Is Reliance 99x better? **Not necessarily.** Size isn't everything.
> Ratios normalise the comparison so you can judge **efficiency, value, and risk** on a level playing field.

### 📌 The 5 Ratios We Cover Today:

| Ratio | What It Measures |
|-------|-----------------|
| **P/E** | How much are you paying per ₹1 of earnings? |
| **P/B** | How much are you paying vs book value? |
| **ROE** | How efficiently is equity being used to generate profit? |
| **ROCE** | How efficiently is ALL capital (debt + equity) being used? |
| **Debt/Equity** | Is the company safe or dangerously leveraged? |

## 📖 LESSON 2 — P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)

### 🧑‍🏫 Master Says:

This is the **most famous ratio in investing.** Everyone talks about it. Most people misuse it.

### Formula:
```
P/E Ratio = Market Price per Share ÷ Earnings per Share (EPS)
```

### What Does It Tell You?

> If P/E = 20, it means: **"You are paying ₹20 for every ₹1 the company earns."**
>
> Or another way: **"It will take 20 years of earnings to recover your investment price"** — assuming earnings stay flat.

### 🔍 Real Example:

| Company | Share Price | EPS | P/E Ratio |
|---------|------------|-----|-----------|
| TCS | ₹3,800 | ₹114 | **33x** |
| Coal India | ₹460 | ₹60 | **7.6x** |

> ❓ Is TCS overpriced? Is Coal India a steal?
> **Not necessarily!** P/E only makes sense when compared to:
> 1. **Industry average** — compare TCS to other IT companies, not Coal India
> 2. **Company's own history** — is current P/E higher or lower than its 5-year average?
> 3. **Growth rate** — a high P/E is justified if growth is also high (see PEG ratio below)

### 🔑 P/E Benchmarks (Indian Market):

| Sector | Typical P/E Range |
|--------|------------------|
| IT (TCS, Infosys) | 25–35x |
| FMCG (HUL, Nestlé) | 50–70x |
| PSU Banks | 6–12x |
| Private Banks | 15–25x |
| Pharma | 20–35x |

### ⚠️ P/E Traps to Avoid:
- **Low P/E ≠ Cheap** — It may be low because the company is dying (Value Trap)
- **High P/E ≠ Expensive** — A fast-growing company deserves a premium P/E
- **P/E is useless for loss-making companies** (negative EPS → meaningless P/E)

### 🎓 Bonus — PEG Ratio:
```
PEG = P/E ÷ Earnings Growth Rate (%)
PEG < 1 = Potentially undervalued even with high P/E ✅
PEG > 2 = Expensive relative to growth 🔴
```

## 📖 LESSON 3 — P/B Ratio (Price-to-Book)

### 🧑‍🏫 Master Says:

Remember from Session 02 — **Book Value** is the net worth of the company (Assets − Liabilities).

**P/B Ratio** tells you: **"How many times are you paying over the company's net worth?"**

### Formula:
```
P/B Ratio = Market Price per Share ÷ Book Value per Share
```

### What Does It Tell You?

| P/B Value | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **P/B < 1** | Stock trading BELOW book value — potential bargain (or the business is terrible!) |
| **P/B = 1** | You're paying exactly what the company is worth on paper |
| **P/B = 3** | You're paying 3x the book value — market expects strong future growth |
| **P/B = 10+** | Very high — market has huge confidence in brand/earnings power |

### 🔍 Real Example — Why P/B Is High for Quality Companies:

> **HUL (Hindustan Unilever)** — P/B of ~50x
> Does this mean it's insanely overpriced?
>
> **No!** HUL's brand (Dove, Surf Excel, Lifebuoy) has enormous value that doesn't appear on the Balance Sheet. The market is paying for **intangible value** — brand, moat, distribution network.
>
> This is called **"Goodwill"** or **"Intangible Assets"** — and it's why P/B alone can mislead you for brand-heavy businesses.

### 🔑 P/B Best Used For:
- **Banks & Financial companies** (where assets = loans, which are measurable)
- **Asset-heavy industries** (Steel, Cement, Real Estate)
- **Avoid using P/B alone** for IT and FMCG (where brand value isn't on Balance Sheet)

## 📖 LESSON 4 — ROE & ROCE

### 🧑‍🏫 Master Says:

These two ratios answer one critical question: **"Is the management team using YOUR money wisely?"**

---

### ROE — Return on Equity

```
ROE = Net Profit ÷ Shareholders' Equity × 100
```

> **Translation:** For every ₹100 of shareholder money invested in the business, how many rupees of profit did management generate?

| ROE | Interpretation |
|-----|---------------|
| Below 10% | Poor — even Fixed Deposits beat this |
| 10–15% | Average |
| 15–20% | Good |
| **Above 20%** | **Excellent — Warren Buffett's minimum threshold** |

### 🔍 Real Example:
> TCS ROE (FY2024) ≈ **48%**
> For every ₹100 shareholders gave TCS, management generated ₹48 of profit. That is exceptional!

---

### ROCE — Return on Capital Employed

```
ROCE = EBIT ÷ Capital Employed × 100
(Capital Employed = Total Assets − Current Liabilities)
```

> **Translation:** For every ₹100 of ALL capital used (including borrowed money), how much operating profit did the company generate?

### ROE vs ROCE — The Critical Difference:

| Metric | Measures | Includes Debt? |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| **ROE** | Profit on shareholder money only | ❌ No |
| **ROCE** | Profit on ALL capital used | ✅ Yes |

> 🔴 **Important Warning:** A company can have **high ROE but terrible ROCE** if it uses a lot of debt to inflate ROE. This is called **Financial Leverage** — and it's dangerous.
>
> **Always check both.** If ROCE < ROE significantly → the company is using debt to boost ROE artificially.

### 🔑 Master's Rule:
> **ROCE should always be greater than the cost of borrowing (interest rate).**
> If ROCE = 8% but the company borrows at 10% interest → every rupee borrowed is **destroying value**!

## 📖 LESSON 5 — Debt-to-Equity Ratio

### 🧑‍🏫 Master Says:

This is the **safety check ratio.** It tells you how much of the company is funded by debt vs shareholders' own money.

### Formula:
```
Debt-to-Equity (D/E) = Total Debt ÷ Shareholders' Equity
```

### What Does It Tell You?

| D/E Ratio | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **0** | Zero debt — ultra-safe (TCS, Infosys) |
| **0 – 0.5** | Very safe — low leverage |
| **0.5 – 1** | Moderate — manageable |
| **1 – 2** | High — watch carefully |
| **Above 2** | Danger zone — small profit drop can cause crisis 🔴 |

### 🔍 Why Debt Is Dangerous:

> Imagine two companies. Both earn ₹100 crore EBIT.
> - Company Safe: Zero debt → All ₹100 crore is profit
> - Company Risky: Debt of ₹500 crore at 15% interest → Interest = ₹75 crore → Only ₹25 crore left as profit
>
> Now if revenue drops 20% in a bad year:
> - Company Safe: ₹80 crore EBIT → still profitable ✅
> - Company Risky: ₹80 crore EBIT − ₹75 crore interest = **₹5 crore profit** → one more bad quarter and they default 🔴

### ⚠️ Exception — Capital-Intensive Industries:
> Banks, NBFCs, Infrastructure, Real Estate companies **normally carry high debt** by nature of their business. For these, use different metrics (NIM, Capital Adequacy Ratio, etc.). D/E is most relevant for **manufacturing and services companies.**

## 📖 LESSON 6 — EPS (Earnings Per Share)

### 🧑‍🏫 Master Says:

EPS is the simplest and most direct measure of how much a company earns **for YOU — the shareholder.**

### Formula:
```
EPS = Net Profit (PAT) ÷ Total Number of Shares
```

### What Does It Tell You?

> If you hold **100 shares** of a company with **EPS = ₹50**, then the company earned **₹5,000** on your behalf this year.
> (Whether it pays it as dividend or reinvests it for growth is a separate decision.)

### 🔍 Real Example — TCS FY2024:
> - Net Profit = ₹45,908 crore
> - Total Shares = ~36.6 crore
> - **EPS = ₹45,908 ÷ 36.6 = ₹125 per share**

### Why EPS Growth Matters More Than EPS Level:

| Year | EPS | Growth |
|------|-----|--------|
| FY2020 | ₹80 | — |
| FY2021 | ₹85 | +6% |
| FY2022 | ₹95 | +12% |
| FY2023 | ₹110 | +16% |
| FY2024 | ₹125 | +14% |

> **Consistent EPS growth = Consistent wealth creation for shareholders.**
> If EPS is growing 15% every year → your investment doubles roughly every 5 years (Rule of 72).

### 🔑 Rule of 72 — Master's Favourite:
```
Years to Double = 72 ÷ Growth Rate (%)
At 15% EPS growth → 72 ÷ 15 = 4.8 years to double 🚀
At 7% growth      → 72 ÷ 7  = 10.3 years to double
At 3% (FD rate)   → 72 ÷ 3  = 24 years to double 😴
```

## 🗒️ Master's Cheat Sheet — Ratio Quick Reference

| Ratio | Formula | Healthy Range | Red Flag |
|-------|---------|---------------|----------|
| **P/E** | Price ÷ EPS | Sector-relative | Negative EPS / extreme outlier |
| **P/B** | Price ÷ Book Value/Share | < 3 for value | > 10 without strong moat |
| **ROE** | Net Profit ÷ Equity × 100 | > 15% | < 10% consistently |
| **ROCE** | EBIT ÷ Capital Employed × 100 | > Cost of Debt | ROCE < Interest Rate |
| **D/E** | Total Debt ÷ Equity | < 1 | > 2 (non-financial) |
| **EPS** | PAT ÷ Total Shares | Growing YoY | Declining or flat |
